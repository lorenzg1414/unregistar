Color Energy Guide

FORM P-06 — CHROMATIC ENERGY DIRECTIVE
What each color does, according to studies the Bureau has not personally re-run. Deploy accordingly.
RedBold. Arousing. Dominant.

Red produces measurable physical effects: raised heart rate, heightened attention, increased perceived attractiveness. Use it when a reaction is the goal — negotiations, dates, bedrooms.

OrangeWarm. Energetic. Sociable.

Orange stimulates like red but reads as friendly rather than threatening. It encourages conversation and lengthens social gatherings. It is not perceived as formal or authoritative.

YellowOptimistic. Vivid. Loud.

Yellow is the most visible color to the human eye — the reason it marks taxis, hazards, and warnings. It is associated with cheerfulness and, in some cultures, luck. Effective as an accent; fatiguing in quantity.

GreenCalming. Balanced. Restorative.

Green is the color of nature, and exposure to it measurably reduces stress and visual fatigue. It suits spaces and days dedicated to recovery. The effect is strongest at the source: go outside.

BlueTrustworthy. Calm. Competent.

Blue is the most preferred color across nearly all cultures studied. It lowers arousal and increases perceived reliability. Use it for interviews, difficult conversations, and sleep. The other party does not need to be informed.

VioletCreative. Rare. Unconventional.

Violet dye was once more expensive than gold, and the association with luxury remained. It signals imagination and independence. Use it when standing out should look deliberate.

PinkGentle. Warm. Disarming.

Pink lowers perceived threat before a word is spoken. It reads as soft, which makes it strategically useful: apologies, first meetings, requests likely to be refused.

BlackPowerful. Formal. Resolute.

Black increases perceived authority, seriousness, and — in some studies — aggression. Use it for boundaries, deadlines, and occasions where you intend to be taken at your word.

WhiteClean. Open. Neutral.

White signals clarity and simplicity. It functions as a visual reset — for a room, a wardrobe, or a week. Any day may be declared day one.
